## The problem with capability as a category

There is a particular kind of product that knows it is useful but cannot make you feel that usefulness in your body. You download it, you see the features, you understand what it does — and then you use it with the same enthusiasm you bring to filing your taxes. The product is capable. You just don't care about it.

This is the consumer design problem in miniature. Capability is not aspirational on its own. What matters is whether the person using the product can see themselves differently because of it. Whether it fits into a story they are already telling about who they are becoming.

All Purpose started from a straightforward observation: the things that help people move better, create better and live better tend to be scattered across categories that don't speak to each other. Fitness is over here. Music is over there. Creative tools are somewhere else entirely. The connections between physical momentum, creative output and the kind of attention that sustains both — those connections don't show up in the products people reach for every day.

The question All Purpose is sitting with, in March 2022, is how to surface those connections in a way that feels like culture rather than curriculum.

## What the directions share

All Purpose is not a single product. It is a cluster of directions that share a common thread. Understanding that thread is the only way to make sense of what the work is pointing at.

Made It Out is the direction most explicitly concerned with creative identity. The premise is simple enough: the people who make things — who write, produce, build, perform — often exist in conditions that don't acknowledge or support what they're doing. Made It Out is interested in that gap. Not as a problem to solve with better features, but as a reality to design for honestly. What does a product look like that takes the creative person seriously, in the full texture of their life?

All Purpose Music sits in a different register. The interest here is in music not just as content but as a companion to creative and physical states. How people use music when they are running, when they are working, when they are in flow and when they are forcing themselves out of stagnation. Music products tend to optimise for passive listening. All Purpose Music is thinking about something more active — music as a tool for modulating your own state of mind.

Relay is the communication and coordination thread. It is early, and the specific form it will take is still being worked out, but the underlying question is about how people who are doing creative work coordinate with each other in ways that don't interrupt the work. Most communication tools are built for office-worker attention patterns — they assume you can stop what you are doing and respond, that context-switching is cheap, that your time is chunked into meetings and slots. Relay starts from a different assumption.

Horizon is the direction concerned with longer arcs. Where All Purpose Music and Made It Out are oriented around the immediate — the session, the moment, the creative act — Horizon is thinking about trajectory. What does someone who is twenty-three and trying to build a life around their creative work actually need to understand about where they are going? What does planning look like when the path isn't a ladder?

None of these directions are finished products. They are orientations. What they share is a refusal to separate capability from identity, and a recognition that the consumer context is fundamentally different from the B2B context that Orbit operates in.

## Why the consumer surface is harder

The Orbit problem — building an operating system for commercial execution — is a hard problem. But it is a problem where the user comes to the product with clear intent. They want to close a deal, manage a workflow, move a project through a stage. The product either serves that intent or it doesn't.

The All Purpose problem is harder in a specific way. The user often cannot name what they want. They know they want to create more, move more, feel less stuck. They know they are not living at the level they could be. But they don't come to a product with a task ready to hand. They come with a mood, a vague dissatisfaction, a sense that something could be different.

Consumer products that work for this kind of user don't explain their value — they embody it. The experience itself has to communicate something. The aesthetic has to signal something. The way you encounter the product for the first time has to make you feel that it understands where you're trying to go, before you've told it anything.

This is the design challenge All Purpose is taking seriously: how do you make capability feel aspirational rather than prescriptive? How do you build something that says "you can move better, create better, live better" without turning that into a motivational poster?

The answer, in March 2022, is still being worked out. But the working hypothesis is that it has more to do with taste than with features. The products that manage it tend to have an aesthetic that people want to be associated with. Not because the aesthetic is flashy, but because it is specific. It says something about what the product values — and by extension, what it expects of you.

## Culture is not a side project

There is a temptation in product development to treat culture — the aesthetic, the tone, the feel — as something you add later, once the core functionality is solid. Get the features right first, then worry about how it looks and feels.

All Purpose is starting from the other end. The cultural texture is not decoration on top of capability. It is the signal that makes capability legible to the kind of person the product is for.

This matters practically. The people All Purpose is thinking about — people trying to build creative lives, to take their physical and mental performance seriously, to operate with more intention — are not drawn in by capability claims. They are drawn in by products that clearly come from a perspective. Products that have a point of view about how things should be.

CheekyGains, which sits within the All Purpose ecosystem as the fitness and performance direction, is an example of this approach taken seriously. The question there is not only "how do we help people train effectively?" It is "what kind of relationship do you have with your own standards?" That is a different question. It shifts the design problem from optimisation to identity — which is where consumer products actually live.

Naira, the AI coaching layer inside CheekyGains, is an early exploration of what it looks like to build a system that adapts to someone's actual state rather than their theoretical goals. The difference matters. Most fitness products are built around what you said you wanted when you signed up. Naira is built around what is actually true of you right now — your energy, your consistency, the gap between what you intended and what you did.

## What generative tools change (and what they don't)

It would be wrong to write about creative culture in March 2022 without acknowledging what is happening with generative models. Image generation has improved to a point where it is practically useful. Text generation is becoming genuinely capable. Music-adjacent tools are emerging. The creative workflow is being restructured in real time.

The temptation — in product thinking and in culture broadly — is to interpret this as a flood of capability that lowers the barrier for everyone equally. And in one sense, it does. The distance between having an idea and having a draft of that idea is shrinking.

But the part that doesn't change is taste. The question of whether something is worth making, whether it says what you actually mean, whether it is shaped in a way that earns a listener or a reader's attention — these are not problems that models solve. They are the specifically human problems that become more important as the production of material becomes cheaper.

The useful framing, for All Purpose, is that generative tools are interesting insofar as they help the person using them move further into what they are actually trying to do. The creative direction, the aesthetic judgment, the sense of when something is finished — these remain the scarce inputs. Speed of production is useful when it frees up more time for judgment. It is not useful when it substitutes for it.

This is not a critique of the tools. It is a design constraint. The All Purpose question is always: does this help someone become more capable, more honest about what they're making, more alive to what they're actually trying to do? If yes, it belongs. If it is just faster noise, it doesn't.

## A working model for the consumer surface

All Purpose is not trying to be everything for everyone who makes things. It is trying to be specific — to have a point of view about what it means to move well, create well, live well — and to express that point of view through products that feel like they come from a coherent culture.

The directions — Made It Out, All Purpose Music, Relay, Horizon — are different expressions of that culture, aimed at different moments in a person's creative and physical life. What connects them is not a feature set. It is a set of commitments about what the people using the products deserve, and what the products owe them in return.

In March 2022, those commitments are still being tested against the reality of building things. What the work is teaching, slowly, is that the consumer surface rewards specificity more than breadth. A product that clearly knows what it is for — and what it is not for — tends to earn more trust than one that tries to cover every case.

That lesson is not unique to All Purpose. But it is one that consumer work keeps having to relearn.
