## The problem with creative people is that they hate process

Which is, of course, exactly backwards.

The best creative work does not happen despite structure — it happens because of it. The resistance to process is usually a resistance to the wrong kind of process: the kind imported from manufacturing or management consulting, built to eliminate variance rather than encourage it. When you apply those systems to creative work, you do not get order. You get paralysis dressed up as professionalism.

The question is not whether creative work needs a workflow. It does. The question is what kind of workflow fits the actual rhythm of creative work — and that rhythm is genuinely different from commercial execution, from product delivery, from operational management.

Getting this distinction wrong is expensive. Not just in quality terms, but in time. Teams either under-structure and spend months on exploration that should have taken weeks, or they over-structure and produce technically competent work that feels empty. The right workflow does neither. It contains the work without killing what makes the work worth doing.

## What creative rhythm actually looks like

Commercial execution is largely linear. You define the outcome, break it into tasks, assign them, and deliver against them. The interesting problems are usually logistical. The goal itself is rarely in question.

Creative work is different in a specific way: the goal itself is part of what you are discovering. You do not start with a complete definition of what the output should be. You start with a direction and a set of instincts, and you move towards something that you will recognise when you find it. This is not vagueness — it is the actual nature of the work.

This means creative workflows need to accommodate three things that commercial workflows often suppress. They need space for exploration that is not immediately evaluable against a fixed target. They need iteration that revisits decisions made earlier, including decisions that seemed settled. And they need subjective evaluation — which means the person running the workflow has to be capable of making taste judgements, not just progress judgements.

None of this is soft. It just operates at a different register. A film editor who can look at a rough cut and identify exactly where the rhythm breaks is doing something as precise as any engineering task. The precision is just harder to codify in a ticket system.

## Five stages that hold together

There is a rough shape to most creative workflows that works, provided each stage is given its actual purpose rather than a compressed version of it.

**Idea capture** comes first. This is the least glamorous part of the process and the most important. The brief, the reference material, the constraints, the emotional tone you are trying to hit — all of it needs to be documented before work begins. Not because creativity works from a checklist, but because half the arguments that happen mid-project are actually arguments about what was or was not captured at the start. Getting explicit early means fewer costly corrections late.

**Exploration** follows, and this is the stage most creative workflows mismanage. It needs a real boundary — a defined window in which you are allowed to go wide without committing — and it needs to actually close. Exploration that does not end is not exploration, it is avoidance. Within the window, rough sketches, experiments that fail, directions that get abandoned: all of this is the work. Outside the window, it is a problem.

**Production** is the stage that people tend to conflate with everything. But production has a specific character: it is the conversion of a chosen direction into a finished artefact. It is not the place to relitigate the direction. Good production discipline means trusting the decisions made in exploration and executing against them with care. Changes in direction during production are the single most common source of creative project failures — not because the new direction is wrong, but because the workflow has no mechanism for absorbing the cost.

**Review** is where subjective evaluation lives, and it is genuinely hard to systematise without destroying it. What you can do is be clear about who the evaluators are, what register they are evaluating in — aesthetic, strategic, audience-fit, technical quality — and what constitutes a decision rather than an opinion. The worst creative reviews are the ones where everyone has a view and nobody has authority. The best are the ones where the evaluation criteria were established before the work was seen.

**Distribution** is the stage that gets neglected most in contexts where creative work is seen as the end rather than the beginning. But the work does not exist until it is in front of the people it was made for. The distribution plan is part of the creative workflow, not an afterthought that production hands off to somewhere else.

## Supporting creativity without smothering it

The failure mode in creative organisations is usually one of two things. Either the workflow is so light that it provides no support — creative people burn hours on exploration that goes nowhere, on review cycles that spin in circles, on distribution that happens whenever someone gets round to it. Or the workflow is so heavy that the creative energy gets processed out of the work entirely — everything evaluated on deliverable quality before the work has had time to breathe.

The solution is not a midpoint between these two. It is a different framing altogether.

A creative workflow's job is not to control the work. Its job is to protect it. To create the conditions in which the work can get done: clear boundaries on exploration, clean handoffs between stages, evaluation criteria established before the work is seen rather than improvised in response to it. Structure as a container, not a cage.

This requires a specific kind of leadership to operate well. Someone who understands both the creative and operational dimensions of the work — who can hold a stage boundary without breaking the creative energy, who can call a review that produces decisions rather than discussions, who can read when a project is genuinely stuck versus when it is doing the slow underground work that good projects sometimes need.

## Where this matters in practice

At MSG, the creative dimension shows up across several different contexts. All Purpose and CheekyGains both operate in spaces where the product needs to carry cultural weight, not just functional utility. A fitness platform or a consumer app that works but does not feel right will not hold an audience. The experience itself is the product, which means the creative process is not adjacent to the product work — it is inseparable from it.

What we have been working on in early 2017 is understanding what the right workflow looks like for this kind of work inside an organisation that is also doing serious operational and technical work simultaneously. The creative arm and the engineering arm need different cadences. Bridging them without forcing either into the other's structure is a genuine organisational design problem, not just a scheduling question.

The answer we keep coming back to is clarity at the stage level. Creative teams do not need to adopt engineering workflows. Engineering teams do not need to adopt creative instincts. But both need to be able to communicate at handoff points — which means both need a shared vocabulary for what stage a piece of work is in, what is being evaluated and by whom, and when a decision has been made.

Without that, the two worlds talk past each other. With it, the work gets better on both sides.

## Process that serves rather than substitutes

There is a version of this thinking that becomes another kind of avoidance — a belief that if you can just find the right workflow, the hard creative work will take care of itself. It will not. The workflow does not write the song or shoot the scene or design the system. It creates the conditions in which someone with skill and judgement can do that work at a higher level of consistency.

Creative work still requires taste. It still requires the willingness to make a decision under uncertainty. It still requires knowing when something is good even before you can explain why, and knowing when it is not good even when everyone around you has convinced themselves otherwise.

What the workflow does is remove the organisational friction that erodes that capacity over time. The creative decisions that matter most are hard enough without also fighting over who is evaluating what and when something is actually finished.

Getting the workflow right does not make creative work easy. It just means that when the work is hard, the difficulty is in the right place.
