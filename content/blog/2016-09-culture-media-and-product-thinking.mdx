## The product instinct applied elsewhere

There is a particular way of looking at a problem that product people develop over time. It is not the same as analytical thinking, though it borrows from it. It is not the same as creative thinking, though it shares some of its reflexes. It is a discipline of asking: who is this for, what does it do for them, how does it reach them, what do we learn from how they respond, and what changes next?

That discipline is mostly spoken of in the context of software. Apps, platforms, dashboards, onboarding flows. But the question worth sitting with, in September 2016, is whether the same frame applies to culture itself — to music, to media, to the kinds of things that shape how people see the world.

The answer is yes. And taking that seriously changes how creative work should be approached.

## What a record label knew that Silicon Valley forgot

Record labels, at their best, were product organisations. They understood audience segmentation before that term existed in technology circles. They knew that an artist released into the wrong market, through the wrong format, in the wrong sequence, would fail regardless of the quality of the underlying work. They spent enormous energy on distribution — not because distribution was more important than the music, but because without it the music simply did not exist for most people.

Radio promotion was a feedback mechanism. Sales figures by region told A&R teams what was landing and where. The chart position of a single relative to the album cycle dictated sequencing decisions. None of this was guesswork dressed as creativity. It was rigorous thinking applied to a creative surface.

The insight did not hold through the digital transition. When the distribution problem appeared to collapse — when anyone could upload anything to anywhere and call it released — the scaffolding that connected creative output to audience attention was dismantled. What replaced it was mostly noise. The internet made distribution near-frictionless, but the consequence was that distribution alone became meaningless. The bottleneck shifted upstream, to attention itself, and nobody had quite rebuilt the discipline for navigating it.

The music industry had encoded product thinking across its whole operating structure — A&R, marketing, promotion, tour support, sync licensing — and the digital era disrupted the structure without replacing the function. The function mattered. It was just lost in the chaos.

## Format is a product decision

One of the things that gets treated as purely aesthetic in media is format. The length of a piece. Whether something is a single or an EP or an album. Whether a piece of writing is 400 words or 4,000. Whether a video runs two minutes or twelve. These feel like creative instincts, and sometimes they are. But format is also a product decision, and the two are not in conflict.

Format shapes distribution. A twelve-minute track does not get radio play regardless of its quality. A 4,000-word essay is not consumed at the pace of a tweet thread regardless of its depth. A film with no theatrical runtime does not get a cinema release. The format encodes assumptions about the channel, the context, the attention available and the audience expectation. Ignoring those assumptions is not creative freedom — it is a failure to understand the medium.

The most effective creative work in any period tends to be work that understands its format constraints deeply enough to either work within them precisely or break them in ways that feel inevitable rather than arbitrary. Both require the same knowledge. You cannot subvert a constraint you do not understand.

Format also governs iteration. A music artist who releases one album every four years has a feedback cycle measured in years. An artist releasing mixtapes, singles and short-form content in regular cadence — deliberately or not — has a faster cycle. The faster cycle provides more signal. Signal enables adjustment. This is the same logic that drives iteration cycles in software. It is not inherently more commercial; it is structurally more capable of learning.

## Audience is not the same as market

There is a phrase that circulates in media circles that does enormous damage: "making things for yourself." The idea is that authentic creative work should be insulated from the preferences of an audience, because thinking about the audience corrupts the work. There is something real in the instinct — work that is merely reactive to trends produces thin, derivative output. But the conclusion drawn from it is usually wrong.

Making things for yourself, when taken seriously as a discipline, requires an unusually precise understanding of your own aesthetic standards and what failure looks like for you. Most people who invoke this phrase have not done that work. They have simply declined to think about audience at all, which is not the same thing.

Understanding your audience is not the same as surrendering to it. It is the same distinction as the difference between a user-centred product and a market-follower product. A user-centred product thinks hard about what the person actually needs, including needs they have not articulated. A market-follower product watches what is selling and copies the surface features. The former requires engagement with the audience as real people with real problems. The latter requires only a spreadsheet.

The most durable creative work tends to come from people who know exactly who they are making things for, have thought carefully about what those people's lives look and feel like, and are making something that meets a real condition in those lives — not a trend, not a demographic projection, but a genuine human state. That is not selling out. It is craft.

## The distribution problem has not been solved

It is tempting, in 2016, to believe the distribution problem has been solved. Streaming has collapsed the manufacturing and shipping cost of music to zero. Social platforms allow direct audience access without gatekeepers. YouTube, SoundCloud and Bandcamp have made the act of release entirely unconstrained.

What has actually happened is that the cost of getting something into existence has fallen to near zero, whilst the cost of getting it seen has increased substantially. The economics of attention are now the dominant economics of creative media, and they are brutal. Platforms surface what already has momentum. Algorithms amplify what is already working. The first thousand listeners are harder to find, not easier, because they are competing with everything that has ever been made and is now permanently accessible.

This means distribution has not been solved — it has been restructured. The question is no longer "can we get this pressed and into shops?" It is "how do we create the conditions for the right people to encounter this work at the right time?" That is a harder question. It does not yield to simple answers, and it cannot be resolved by uploading something and waiting.

Product thinking helps here not because it has the answers but because it insists on the right questions. Who specifically will encounter this first? Through what channel? What will they experience? What do we need to be true about that experience for them to share it? What can we learn from what happens next?

## Creative work is also product work

There is an impulse to defend creative work from the language of product development, as if importing rigour from one domain will drain meaning from the other. That is a false trade. Rigour does not replace taste. Analysis does not replace instinct. The musician who tracks which songs consistently produce emotional responses in live settings and makes decisions about future direction based on that observation is not compromising their art — they are taking it seriously enough to learn from it.

For the All Purpose ecosystem, this question sits close to actual work. The music, the content, the media output — all of it can be approached with the same questions that govern software: Who is this for? Does it serve a real condition in their lives? How does it reach them? What do we learn from how they respond? What changes next?

This is not a framework imposed from outside. It is a way of taking the work seriously enough to want it to land, and to care about whether it does.

## Iteration as respect

There is a final point worth making plainly. Treating creative work as something that iterates — something that improves through feedback cycles, through release and response and adjustment — is not a devaluation of the work. It is a form of respect for it.

The alternative is to treat each piece of creative work as a monument: finished, fixed, beyond revision and learning. That posture might feel principled, but it is mostly defensive. It protects the creator from the discomfort of learning that the work did not do what was intended, at the cost of ever making work that actually does.

Product thinking applied to culture is not about making culture more commercial. It is about making it more rigorous, more honest about what it is trying to achieve, and more capable of achieving it. The discipline transfers. The creative integrity does not need to be surrendered in exchange.

What is actually at stake is whether the people doing this work treat it with the same care they would bring to anything they genuinely want to see exist in the world. The answer should not be in doubt.
