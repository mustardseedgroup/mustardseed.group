There is a conversation that rarely happens in product development. Teams spend enormous energy on features, on retention loops, on onboarding flows and notification strategies. They map the moments where a user might drop off and engineer the moments where they might stay. But they rarely sit down and ask: what kind of person does this product quietly encourage someone to become?

That is a cultural question. And it turns out culture is a product surface — one of the most consequential ones, and one of the least consciously designed.

## The product that runs underneath the product

When people talk about culture in the context of companies, they usually mean internal culture: how a team operates, what behaviours get rewarded, how decisions get made under pressure. That is worth thinking about carefully. But there is a second sense of culture that matters just as much for product work — the culture that an app creates among its users.

Think about the social patterns that a platform shapes over time. Not through a single feature, but through the accumulated effect of many small design decisions: who gets visibility, what kind of activity gets rewarded, what tone the interface sets, what behaviour the incentive structure quietly encourages. The cultural output of a product is the sum of all these signals, operating continuously, usually below the level of conscious attention.

Most products do not design this intentionally. The cultural effect emerges anyway — from the mechanics of engagement, from what gets surfaced in a feed, from what the platform treats as success. The culture is shaped whether or not anyone chose to shape it. The only question is whether the shaping was deliberate.

## All Purpose as a design question

All Purpose began with a set of questions about what a creative culture could look like when it was built rather than inherited. The inherited version of creative culture — the one you absorb from the industry, from comparison, from social media dynamics — tends to reward output volume, visibility and external validation. You make things so others can see that you made them. The feedback loop runs through likes and streams and follower counts, which are themselves shaped by algorithmic dynamics that have little to do with the quality of what was made or the integrity of the person making it.

That culture is not neutral. It shapes how people relate to their own creativity. It encourages a particular kind of relationship with the work — one mediated by how it performs publicly rather than what it does for the person who made it. Over time, it can erode the intrinsic motivation that made someone want to create in the first place.

The question All Purpose started with was whether a different culture was possible. Not as an aspiration, but as something designed — deliberately engineered into the mechanics of how people relate to the platform, to performance and to each other.

That meant thinking about culture the way you think about a product: identify the outputs you want, work backwards to the inputs and mechanics that produce them, and iterate towards better results. Culture as something that can be shaped, not just inherited or complained about.

## What shaping culture actually requires

Designing culture is not the same as writing values on a wall. It requires thinking carefully about feedback loops — what behaviour the system rewards, at what pace, and with what kind of signal.

A fitness product that only surfaces numbers (weight, reps, times) trains a particular relationship to the body: transactional, metric-first, oriented towards output. A product that also surfaces qualitative reflection, that creates space for effort and consistency rather than just result, trains a different one. The mechanics do not just measure the culture — they produce it.

For a creative product, the equivalent question is: what does the platform treat as progress? If progress means reaching a larger audience, you get one set of behaviours. If progress means deepening a practice, or making something technically harder, or completing a thing you started, you get a different set. The definition of success embedded in the product mechanics is also a definition of creative value — and it gets absorbed by the people using the product, whether or not they notice.

This is why product design for All Purpose has never been only about features. It has been about the shape of the culture the features collectively produce. CheekyGains, as the performance and accountability layer within the All Purpose ecosystem, carries this logic into the physical domain. The question there is not only what someone lifts or runs — it is what relationship to capability and standard they build over time. The product is trying to produce something durable, not just engagement.

## The opportunity created by intentional design

There is a strong case to be made that the most culturally significant consumer products of the next decade will be the ones that take this design question seriously. We are at a particular moment where the dominant cultural platforms — the ones that have shaped creative behaviour at scale — are facing genuine scepticism about whether their incentive structures serve the people using them. That scepticism is not going away. If anything, it will deepen as the effects of those structures become more visible and more documented.

Into that space, products that are designed around a coherent cultural thesis have a real opportunity. Not because they can necessarily compete at the same scale immediately, but because they are building something that the dominant platforms are structurally unable to build. A platform optimised for engagement cannot easily optimise simultaneously for creative integrity, because those two objectives often pull in opposite directions. A product that starts from cultural design rather than retrofitting it has an advantage that compounds over time.

That advantage is earned slowly. You cannot announce a culture — you can only build the mechanics that produce it, then maintain them consistently enough for the culture to actually form. The people who experience it carry it; that is the only propagation mechanism that works.

## Responsibility and the quiet curriculum

If culture is a product surface, then product teams carry a responsibility that most of them have not fully reckoned with. Every design decision is a small lesson in what to value, how to relate to others, what counts as achievement. Multiplied across millions of interactions, these lessons constitute something like a curriculum — one that operates below explicit instruction and below conscious awareness.

That word is not hyperbole. The consistent framing of creative work as public performance is a lesson. The consistent reward of novelty over depth is a lesson. The treatment of attention as the primary currency of value is a lesson. None of these lessons need to be written down anywhere to be taught effectively. The product teaches them simply by being the context in which people spend time and make decisions.

Taking this seriously means holding two things in mind at once. First, that the opportunity is real: you can design towards a culture of genuine capability, genuine creativity, genuine relationship to quality. Products built around that north star can do something distinctly valuable. Second, that the responsibility is real: building something that shapes how people relate to their own creative lives is not a trivial undertaking, and the failure modes are not trivial either.

The question for All Purpose, held from the beginning and still worth asking: does the mechanic we are building help someone become more capable, more honest about their work, more connected to why they make things? Or does it route them back towards comparison and external validation, just with a different logo on the interface?

That question does not always have a clean answer. But the fact that it is being asked — and that the answer can change what gets built — is what makes culture a genuine design surface rather than a side effect.
