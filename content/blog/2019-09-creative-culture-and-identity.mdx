## The identity you perform before you believe it

There is something that happens when a person enters a creative community — a band, a studio, a writing collective, a gym where serious athletes train. They do not simply absorb skills. They begin to see themselves differently. They start carrying the identity before it fully belongs to them, and then slowly, through repetition and proximity and evidence, it does.

Social researchers have a name for this. They call it identity-based behaviour: the idea that what people do is governed not only by what they want but by who they believe themselves to be. Habits researchers have observed for years that the most durable behaviour change happens not when someone sets a goal, but when they start performing an identity. The person who says "I'm trying to quit smoking" is in a weaker position than the person who has internalised the frame "I am someone who does not smoke." The second person is defending a self-concept, not just managing a behaviour.

Culture is the environment that produces those self-concepts. Creative communities — scenes, schools, labels, studios — are particularly powerful at it. You join one not knowing whether you belong, and then the community's norms, aesthetics, expectations and shared language slowly fill in who you are inside it. Within a year you are making different choices, holding different standards, noticing different things. This is not incidental. It is the primary output of a creative culture.

## What it means to make someone see themselves as a maker

The question this raises for product work is unusual and worth sitting with. Most consumer products are designed around behaviour: the thing the user is supposed to do. Sign up. Complete the habit. Upload the content. Log the meal. The product lives in flows and friction reduction and engagement metrics.

But if the deepest layer of durable behaviour is identity, then a different question appears: what does this product do to how the user sees themselves? Not what does it do to their behaviour in the app, but what does it do to how they think about themselves when the app is closed and they are just living?

A product that helps someone see themselves as a capable creative person — not as a passive consumer but as a person who makes things, who moves differently through the world because of it — is doing something fundamentally more interesting than a product that optimises for time spent. It is doing something closer to what a community does. It is producing self-concept.

This is hard to build. It requires taste. It requires that the product not feel like a beginner's tool, because beginner framing sends a signal about who belongs. It requires that the content or capability inside the product carry a standard that earns the identity, because a claimed identity without evidence does not stick. And it requires that the feedback loops inside the product affirm the right things — not just engagement, but capability, growth, completion, expression.

## Culture as a product surface

What creative communities do in the physical world — produce identity, form taste, raise standards, hold people accountable to a version of themselves — a product can do in part. Not entirely. A product cannot replace the felt experience of being in a room with people who are serious about something. But it can carry that seriousness outward. It can extend the reach of a culture, preserve its signal, and bring people into contact with a standard they would not have found on their own.

This is what makes culture a genuine product surface, not just a backdrop or an aesthetic. The culture is doing real work. It is shaping what users believe they are capable of and who they believe themselves to be. A product that is indifferent to this — that treats culture as branding rather than as structural — is leaving the most durable form of engagement on the table.

The products we care most about in our own lives tend to be the ones that participate in our self-concept. The instrument you keep even when you are not playing. The book that changed how you thought about your own capability. The community, online or physical, that made you feel as though you had moved up. These are not just products with good UX. They are artefacts of identity formation.

## What All Purpose is trying to do

All Purpose sits inside this question directly. It is a consumer platform, and consumer platforms in 2019 tend to measure themselves by reach and time spent. But the aspiration here is different.

The bet is that there is an underserved group of people who want to be more capable — creatively, physically, intellectually — and who are looking for products that treat them as capable rather than as passive audiences waiting to be entertained. Products that offer a standard rather than just a recommendation. That demand something rather than just deliver something.

CheekyGains inside the All Purpose ecosystem is an example of this orientation. It is a fitness and performance product, and fitness products are saturated. But the majority of fitness products target behaviour — log this, track that, close your rings. CheekyGains is thinking about identity: who does the user believe themselves to be after consistent engagement, and does the product produce someone who carries a higher standard even away from the product itself?

The sub-products within All Purpose — the music, media and culture directions — are being approached with the same lens. Not "what content is popular" but "what content builds something in the person who engages with it." What leaves them with a higher sense of their own creative capacity. What makes them more likely to make something themselves rather than only consume.

That is the harder design target. It is also the more interesting one.

## The taste problem

There is a genuine difficulty here that is worth naming honestly. Producing self-concept in users requires that the product itself embody a clear aesthetic standard. Taste is not neutral. A product can carry a worldview about what quality looks and sounds like, what creativity is for, what constitutes a high standard of execution. When that worldview is coherent and confident, users can enter it and adopt it. When it is fuzzy or lowest-common-denominator, the identity signal it sends is weak.

This is a real constraint. Taste cannot be faked, and it cannot be industrialised without losing what makes it functional. A creative product that is trying to reach a large audience faces the familiar pressure to smooth off edges, remove anything that might alienate, optimise for the broadest possible engagement. But that pressure runs directly against the mechanism that makes identity formation possible. Identity formation requires a standard. A standard requires that some things are better than others. That is a position, not a feature.

Building products that carry genuine taste at scale without diluting that taste into incoherence is one of the real problems in this category. It is not solved by design systems alone, though those help. It is solved by having a clear and honest answer to the question: what kind of person are we for? Not in a demographic sense, but in a values and aspiration sense. What does the ideal user believe about themselves, and does our product affirm that belief or produce it?

## The longer view

The reason identity is worth taking seriously as a design target — not just as a nice-to-have, but as a core thesis — is that it is the only mechanism that produces durable behaviour at scale without constant external pressure.

Products that rely on notifications, streaks and friction patterns to keep users engaged are fighting against attention constantly. They win some days and lose others. Products that have successfully shifted how users see themselves have a different relationship with those users. The user is not fighting the product's retention mechanism. They are returning because the product is part of how they understand themselves.

That is a much harder thing to build. It requires consistency over a long time. It requires that every surface — content, interactions, feedback, social dynamics if any — carry the same signal about who this product is for and what kind of person it produces. But the reward for building it is a relationship with users that is not essentially a battle against their competing attention, but something more like the relationship people have with communities and cultures they genuinely belong to.

That is what we are working towards. Not in a single quarter, not with a single feature. But as the underlying logic of the consumer work inside All Purpose.
