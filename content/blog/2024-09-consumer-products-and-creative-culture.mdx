## Tools do not make culture

There is a version of the current moment that gets told entirely as a tools story. Generative models lower the cost of producing images, music, video, text. Creative output becomes cheaper. More people can make things. Democratisation, the headlines say.

That framing is not wrong. It is just incomplete — and the part it omits is the part that actually determines what gets made and whether it matters.

Culture is not produced by tools. Culture is produced by people who have formed a particular sensibility, a particular set of references, a particular standard for what counts as good. Tools accelerate execution within a sensibility. They do not create the sensibility itself. A generative image model in the hands of someone with genuine visual literacy produces something worth looking at. The same model in the hands of someone who has not developed that literacy produces more content — competent in texture, hollow in meaning.

This distinction matters because the AI era does not change what culture is. It changes the production economics around it. And those two things need to stay clearly separated if you want to build anything that lasts.

## What becomes abundant, and what does not

The AI-assisted creative workflow of 2024 looks roughly like this: a direction, a prompt or a reference, a generated output, an iteration, a refinement. For a lot of surface-level creative work — social assets, draft concepts, exploratory mood boards — this loop is genuinely useful. It compresses the distance between "I have an idea" and "here is a version of the idea I can react to."

What that loop cannot produce on its own is taste. Taste is not a setting. It is an accumulation. It is the result of years of exposure to things that are good, bad, derivative, surprising and quietly correct. It is developed by paying attention to what moves you and what leaves you cold, and building an internal model refined enough that you can explain the difference. Taste is infrastructure that takes a long time to build and cannot be prompted into existence.

In a world where execution is increasingly automated, taste becomes more — not less — valuable. If anyone can produce a competent image, a decent piece of music, a passable edit, then what is left as a point of differentiation is judgement. Knowing which direction is worth pursuing. Knowing when to stop. Knowing what the thing is actually for.

This is not a comfortable conclusion for anyone who assumed that access to tools would level the creative playing field entirely. Tools level the floor. They do not level the ceiling. The ceiling is still set by people who have done the work of developing a genuine point of view.

## Culture as a product surface

Consumer products that touch culture — music platforms, fitness communities, lifestyle apps, media ecosystems — are not just distribution systems. They are environments in which culture is formed.

This is an important distinction. A distribution system is neutral. It moves things from one place to another without shaping them. But platforms, communities and products are not neutral. They have an aesthetic, a set of norms, a standard of what counts as worthy. Even by deciding what they will not host, they are making a cultural statement. There is no neutral container for human creativity and identity.

All Purpose is built with this understood. The ecosystem spans fitness and performance through CheekyGains, music through All Purpose Music, community and media through Relay, personal development through Horizon, and story through Made It Out. These are not a random collection of consumer verticals. They are expressions of a unified argument about what a capable, directed, creatively alive life looks like.

The argument is this: the same person who is serious about their physical performance — who trains, who holds themselves to standards, who pushes through difficulty rather than around it — is often the same person who is serious about their creative output, their community, their story. These things cohere. They are not separate interests wearing the same person. They are facets of a single orientation towards life that values growth, honesty and capability over comfort, image and ease.

A product ecosystem that understands this does not need to manufacture artificial connections between fitness and music or between community and performance. The connection is already there, in the people who are serious about these things. The product's job is to honour that coherence rather than flatten it.

## Naira and the coaching question

CheekyGains introduced Naira, the AI performance coach embedded in the product, as a direct expression of this. The question Naira is trying to answer is not "how do I make fitness content that scales?" — it is "what does it actually mean to coach someone effectively, and can that be done with intelligence and care at a level of personalisation that was previously impossible?"

Coaching is a creative discipline. A good coach does not deliver a standard programme. They read the person in front of them — their history, their psychology, their patterns of avoidance and effort — and they adapt. They hold a standard while meeting someone where they are. That is an art form.

The AI coaching question is whether that art form can be approximated at scale. Not replaced — approximated. The difference matters. An approximation that genuinely serves the person it is coaching is valuable. A replacement that simulates coaching without any real understanding of the individual is just a motivational chatbot with better graphics.

The distinction Naira was built around is the same distinction that runs through all of this: access to the tool is not the point. The quality of the judgement embedded in how the tool is used is the point.

## Standards as the actual product

In September 2024, the broader cultural conversation is dominated by a kind of productivity anxiety. Everyone can produce more. More images, more songs, more posts, more pitches. The pressure to produce is at an all-time high. The pressure to develop taste, to sit with something until it is actually good, to resist the urge to ship the competent version when the excellent version is within reach — that pressure has, if anything, reduced.

This creates a real opportunity for products that hold a standard. Not in a gatekeeping sense — the traditional creative gatekeepers of the last century were often protecting mediocrity as much as they were protecting quality. But in the sense of being honest about what good looks like, and building communities around people who care about the difference.

The fitness world has understood this for a long time. Serious training communities have standards. You cannot half-heartedly attend and claim the same identity as someone who shows up consistently. The culture of a serious gym or a serious sport is partly about accountability — about the group holding a standard that any individual member benefits from even when they would prefer not to be held to it.

That same structure applies to creative communities. A community with a genuine standard, a genuine aesthetic, a genuine point of view, is more valuable to its members than a community that accepts everything because it does not want to exclude anyone. The latter is not a community. It is a distribution list.

All Purpose products are being built for people who want to be held to something. That is both a design choice and a cultural stance. It means the products will appeal strongly to some people and will not be for others. That is the correct outcome. Universal appeal and genuine quality rarely coexist at the consumer level.

## The production side and the cultural side

The working distinction that has been useful in thinking about how generative tools interact with creative work is this: the production side and the cultural side are separate, and they need to be handled separately.

The production side — how quickly you can go from an idea to an executed version of that idea — is genuinely being transformed. Workflows that used to take days take hours. Iterations that required expensive specialists can now be run by small teams with the right tooling and enough taste to direct the output. For products that need to move quickly, that is a real capability shift.

The cultural side — what the work is for, who it is for, what standard it is being held to, what it means in the lives of the people who encounter it — is not being transformed in the same way. These questions are answered by human beings with genuine sensibilities, building genuinely considered things. The models do not help with that. The human infrastructure — the relationships, the creative direction, the community norms, the editorial judgement — still has to be built by hand.

Getting the production side right allows you to move faster. Getting the cultural side right is what determines whether the thing you are moving towards is worth arriving at.

## The longer work

What consumer products actually transmit, over time, is a way of seeing. Not just a set of features. Not just a service. A particular orientation towards experience — what counts as good, what is worth striving for, what kind of person you are becoming by spending your attention here.

The tools of 2024 make it easier to produce more. What they cannot do is substitute for the long work of developing that orientation — building products with genuine values, communities with genuine standards, creative work with genuine care.

That work is slower than it looks, and less impressive at the start than it becomes over time. But it is the only kind that actually shapes culture. Everything else is content.
