## Start with what you can afford to be wrong about

There is a version of ambition that insists on the grand gesture. The big launch. The definitive product. The fully realised vision delivered in one moment. It is a compelling instinct — nothing cheap-feeling, nothing provisional, nothing that signals you did not quite believe in the thing.

That instinct is also frequently wrong, and expensively so.

The alternative is not to lower your ambitions. It is to separate the ambition from the method. You can want to build something significant and still choose to start with the smallest version that teaches you something real. The two are not in conflict. The small experiment is often the only route to the large, durable thing — not a compromise on the way there, but the actual path.

June 2015, for Mustard Seed Group, is a month to be honest about that. We are not yet the institution we intend to become. We are building the conditions for it, testing assumptions before committing resources, and trying to develop the discipline of learning cheaply rather than betting expensively.

## What makes an experiment small — and why that is the point

Small has a specific meaning here. It does not mean amateur or low-quality. It means scoped to a single question, cheap enough to run multiple times, and fast enough that the feedback arrives before you have forgotten what you were asking.

A large bet typically answers the question "did we succeed?" only after the resources are spent. A small experiment answers a narrower question — "is this assumption correct?" — while you still have room to adjust. The distinction sounds obvious when stated plainly, and yet most organisations default to the large bet because it feels more serious. The planning is more involved. The launch is more visible. The team is larger. It carries the weight of commitment.

What it often lacks is learning. Commitment is not the same as knowledge, and spending more money on an incorrect assumption does not correct the assumption — it just makes it costlier.

The small experiment, by contrast, is deliberately modest in scope so that the question it asks is crisp. One variable, one population, one period of time. The answer may be ambiguous — experiments often are — but the process of designing them forces clarity about what you are actually trying to learn. That clarity alone is worth something.

## Feedback cycles and the cost of being wrong

Two things change at meaningful speed when you shorten the feedback cycle. The first is the cost of being wrong. If you have spent three months and significant resources building a product direction before learning that your core assumption was flawed, the cost of that lesson is very high. The work must be undone, the resources cannot be recovered, and the team must recalibrate from a position of exhaustion. If instead you tested the assumption in the first two weeks — cheaply, at small scale — the cost of the same lesson is a fortnight of focused work and whatever you spent to run the test.

The second thing that changes is the rate at which you can update your model of the world. Every experiment that runs and returns a result adds information. Run a hundred small experiments over the course of a year and you have a hundred updates to your understanding of the market, the user, the problem, the product. Run one large bet and you have, if you are lucky, one update. The compounding effect of more frequent feedback is significant — both for the quality of the decisions you make and for the morale of the team doing the work.

There is also something worth saying about what frequent feedback does to risk tolerance. When you are running large bets, the psychological pressure of potential failure is high because the downside is large and distant. When you are running small experiments, the individual downside is small and the cycle is short. You can fail an experiment, learn from it, and run the next one within days. That changes the texture of the work. It becomes easier to be intellectually honest about negative results because a negative result from a small experiment is not a catastrophe — it is just information that updates the next iteration.

## The creative application

This principle is not only commercial. It applies equally to creative work, to product design, to research, and to institution-building.

Creative work that insists on being fully realised before it reaches the world often stagnates. The draft that cannot be shared because it is not yet good enough. The design that cannot be tested because it is not yet complete. The idea that cannot be discussed because it might be stolen or misunderstood. These hesitations — each understandable in isolation — accumulate into a creative practice that produces very little, because nothing is ever quite ready.

The small experiment, applied to creative work, is the prototype you share in week two rather than week twelve. The early draft you circulate before you are attached to it. The concept you test on a small audience before building the full production. The early exposure is uncomfortable — it requires accepting that the thing is incomplete — but the feedback it generates is almost always more useful than the feedback you would have received later, when the attachment is stronger and the revision is harder.

The principle holds in research as well. Rather than spending months developing a comprehensive theoretical framework before testing any of its parts, the more effective approach is to isolate the most uncertain assumption in the framework, run a small test of that assumption specifically, and use the result to sharpen the framework before proceeding. The output is a better framework, built faster, through more iterations of shorter duration.

## The MSG approach in practice

For Mustard Seed Group, this is not an abstract principle. It is a working method.

Before committing significant development time to any product direction, we look for the smallest version of the core assumption that can be tested. What is the question this product direction is actually betting on? Is that assumption testable before we build? If yes, how do we design the smallest test that will give us a meaningful answer?

This applies across the portfolio. For TUXX, working with clients on custom systems, the question is often which workflow assumption the system is built on — and whether that assumption reflects how the client's team actually operates, rather than how they think they operate. The gap between those two is frequently significant, and the cost of discovering it late is high. A small experiment run early — a lightweight prototype, a week of manual process before automating it, a simple tool before a comprehensive one — surfaces that gap while the build is still cheap.

For consumer-facing work in the All Purpose ecosystem, the questions are different but the structure is the same. What is the assumption about user behaviour? Can it be tested in a week with a small cohort before being built into the product? Often, yes. Often, the test produces a different answer than the assumption predicted.

For research at Benediction Lab, small experiments have a specific function: they are the mechanism for narrowing the question. A research question that is too broad will absorb effort without producing anything useful. A small experiment that tests one specific aspect of the broader question produces a result that sharpens the next question. Over time, this narrowing process produces clarity that a single comprehensive study rarely achieves.

## The discipline, not the tactic

The reason this matters at a company level is that small experiments are not just a tactical decision. They represent a particular relationship to uncertainty.

The organisation that defaults to large bets is implicitly claiming confidence it may not have. The confidence might be genuine — sometimes it is warranted — but often it reflects a discomfort with admitting uncertainty, or a cultural expectation that seriousness requires commitment, or a structural incentive where the person proposing the bet is not the person who bears the cost if the assumption is wrong.

The organisation that runs small experiments is doing something different. It is admitting, structurally, that its current beliefs are provisional. That admission requires a particular kind of intellectual honesty, especially for founders who are also operators — who have to be confident enough to act whilst simultaneously remaining genuinely open to being wrong.

That tension does not resolve into a comfortable equilibrium. It is a practice, a discipline maintained over time, not a method you adopt once and have solved.

## What we are building toward

The reason to develop this practice now, at this stage of building Mustard Seed Group, is that the habits formed in the early period become the operating assumptions of the later period. If the instinct is to test first, the organisation inherits that instinct as it grows. If the instinct is to build first and learn later, it inherits that too — and unlearning it is harder than learning the alternative from the start.

Small experiments are not a constraint on ambition. They are a method for pursuing ambitious things without destroying the organisation's capacity to learn whilst doing so.

The mustard seed itself is small. That is precisely the point.
