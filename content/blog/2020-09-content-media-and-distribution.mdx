The question that kept surfacing through 2020 was not whether people were consuming content. It was whether the systems beneath that consumption had fundamentally shifted — and whether anyone building in this space had properly reckoned with what that meant.

The short answer: yes and mostly no.

## What the lockdowns clarified

When physical spaces closed in spring 2020, the media industry did not grind to a halt. It accelerated. Streaming hours climbed. Podcast downloads climbed. Email newsletter subscriptions climbed. Independent creators who had spent years building direct relationships with audiences suddenly looked prescient. Everyone else looked overexposed to platforms they didn't control.

The event clarified something that had been true for a long time but easy to ignore: distribution is not downstream of content. It is not the final step in a creative process that begins with an idea and ends with a publish button. Distribution is the architecture that determines whether a piece of work ever reaches the person it was meant for — and on whose terms.

For years, the dominant model had been to create compelling material and then hand it to a platform. The platform handled discovery. The platform handled the relationship. The platform held the audience data. The creator — or the media company, or the studio — provided the raw material that made the platform worth visiting. What 2020 made visible was how fragile that arrangement had always been.

## The infrastructure problem

Reach without infrastructure is temporary. Every independent journalist who spent the 2010s building a Twitter following had reach. Many of them could not name ten people in that audience. They had no direct channel. When the platform changed an algorithm, their distribution changed. They were renting, not owning.

The media organisations and creators who navigated 2020 with relative stability tended to share a common trait: they had built some form of direct infrastructure before the crisis arrived. Email lists. Member communities. Subscription tiers. Owned apps. In each case, the distribution asset was something they controlled, not something a third-party platform allocated to them based on that week's engagement signals.

This is the distribution thesis that has shaped how we think about the consumer side of All Purpose. The goal has never been simply to make compelling content. The goal is to build the infrastructure through which that content reaches people — and through which the relationship with those people compounds over time.

Distribution infrastructure is harder to build than content. It requires thinking about systems, not just output. But it is the part that creates durable value. Content is the fuel. Infrastructure is the engine.

## Attention has a different shape now

One consequence of 2020 is that audience attention has fractured and deepened simultaneously. Fractured because people have more surfaces to fill — more hours at home, more devices, more scrolling. Deepened because, in the absence of easy social options, some audiences have become genuinely committed to the work they find meaningful.

The era when you could reach a mass audience by placing the right piece of content on the right platform and hoping an algorithm surfaced it is not over, but it is less reliable than it was. What is growing — and growing fast — is the value of depth. Audiences that know what you stand for. Communities built around a point of view rather than a content category. Creators and media organisations that have earned trust rather than just accumulated clicks.

This matters for how All Purpose is being built. The consumer products within that ecosystem — CheekyGains among them — are not trying to generate viral moments. They are trying to accumulate genuine commitments. The difference is architectural: one optimises for reach at a given moment, the other optimises for the durability of the relationship over time.

## Not just what you make, but how it moves

The practical lesson from watching the 2020 media landscape is that you need a position on distribution before you start producing at scale. The production question — what to make, at what quality, in what format — is relatively tractable. Most teams with clear creative direction can work that out. The distribution question is harder because it forces decisions that feel premature when you're starting out but become critical very quickly.

Who owns the relationship with the audience? What happens if the platform you're relying on changes its terms? How do you move someone from a casual listener or reader to a committed participant in the thing you're building? These are not questions that content strategy answers on its own. They require distribution architecture.

The teams that handled 2020 well had typically answered those questions years earlier, often without realising how important the answers would become. The teams that struggled had often deferred the questions — assuming that good work would find its audience, that platform relationships were stable, that the infrastructure question could wait until the content was established.

## Relay and the logic of direct reach

Within All Purpose, Relay is the name for work being done on the direct reach problem — how content, messages and community interactions move between the people building and the people following. The exact product shape is still developing, but the logic behind it is clear.

If you are building something in the consumer space that involves media, performance, creativity or culture — and All Purpose sits across all of those — the distribution layer cannot be an afterthought. It has to be part of the product surface from the start.

The 2020 media landscape accelerated what was already an inevitable reckoning with platform dependency. Independent creators who had diversified their distribution were fine. Those who hadn't were dealing with reach that could be turned off by an algorithm change they had no say in. Building distribution infrastructure is slower and less immediately satisfying than publishing content. It is also the difference between owning your audience and borrowing it.

## What hasn't changed

Amidst everything that shifted this year, one thing held: quality of relationship matters more than volume of reach. The media properties that retained audience trust through 2020 were overwhelmingly the ones that had earned it through clarity, consistency and genuine usefulness — not the ones with the largest follower counts or the most aggressive publishing cadence.

Volume without relationship is hollow. The consumer brands, creators and media organisations that are over-indexed on output and under-invested in the relationship with the people they're making things for will face a reckoning. The distribution landscape has shifted in ways that expose exactly that gap.

All Purpose, as a creative and consumer ecosystem, is being built with that lesson in mind from the start. The ambition is not to generate the most content or accumulate the most passive followers. It is to build a genuine community of people who see themselves in what's being made — and who have a direct, durable relationship with the work.

That starts with making things worth caring about. But it requires having the infrastructure to deliver them directly to the people who care.

## What we're watching

The second half of 2020 will test whether the changes in media consumption habits that appeared in spring are structural or temporary. Early evidence suggests structural. The audiences that shifted to new formats and new platforms during lockdowns are not fully reverting. The independent newsletter and podcast market has absorbed enough new subscribers and listeners that a permanent step-change seems likely.

For anyone building in the consumer and media space, the distribution question should now be front of mind rather than deferred. The window for building owned infrastructure — email, community, direct subscription — on the back of elevated attention is open. It will not stay open indefinitely.

The thesis that shaped how we think about distribution at All Purpose was not invented in 2020. But 2020 has made it harder to argue against.
