There is a version of the creative person that gets held up as a kind of ideal: someone who works in bursts, who is struck by ideas at strange hours, who cannot quite explain where the work comes from. The output is brilliant but the process is opaque, even to them. Inspiration arrives or it doesn't. You wait.

This is a seductive picture. It is also, when you look at it honestly, a fairly useless one.

## The romantic trap

The romantic view of creativity flatters the creator whilst quietly removing their responsibility for the work. If inspiration is the source, then the dry periods are just bad luck. The good periods are gifts. Neither state is really in your hands, which means you cannot be held to any standard of creative production — only celebrated when it happens to flow.

For anyone building something, that is a problem. Not because inspiration is worthless — it is not — but because creative work that only happens when inspiration strikes is creative work you cannot depend on. And an organisation that cannot depend on its creative output is not operating; it is waiting.

The early question at All Purpose was whether creative output could be made repeatable. Whether taste, originality and energy could be structured into a practice rather than left to the weather of individual moods. The answer, after enough experimentation, was yes — but it required dismantling the romantic frame entirely.

## What creativity actually is

Creativity is not a disposition you either have or lack. It is a set of behaviours — the habit of generating options before committing to one, the discipline of working a problem from multiple angles, the practice of producing material before evaluating it. These behaviours can be trained. They can be scheduled. They can be managed.

This is not a radical claim. Artists who are serious about their output have always known it. Painters set studio hours. Writers have word counts. Musicians schedule sessions. The discipline is not the enemy of the work — it is what makes the work possible at scale, across time, across a team.

What changes when you treat creativity as a discipline rather than a disposition is what you can ask of it. You can ask for it on Tuesday at 10am. You can ask for it in service of a specific brief. You can build feedback loops around it and improve it over time. You can hold it to standards.

## Practice, not performance

There is a useful distinction between creative practice and creative performance. Performance is the output — the finished thing, the launch, the campaign, the piece of work someone sees. Practice is everything that builds the muscle: the sketches, the bad first drafts, the research, the iteration, the arguments, the decisions made and unmade.

Most creative organisations spend too much time managing the performance and too little time building the practice. They have a process for approval, a timeline for delivery, a brief to respond to — but no systematic investment in the condition of the people who have to produce the work. When output is weak, they pull levers on the performance side: change the brief, change the deadline, change the team. The practice underneath stays the same.

The argument we kept returning to, early on, was that the practice is the product. The outputs are evidence of the practice, but the thing worth investing in — the thing that compounds — is the creative capacity of the people and the conditions that sustain it.

## Operating vs. possessing

You can possess creativity in the way you can possess a voice, a gift for languages, or a good eye. Possessing it is table stakes. The question is whether you can operate it.

Operating it means: understanding the conditions under which it functions well and building those conditions deliberately. It means knowing when you are in a generative mode and when you are in a critical one, and not mixing them. It means recognising that a team's creative output is a function of inputs — the quality of problems they are given, the time available for exploration, the degree to which early ideas are safe to share — and managing those inputs as seriously as you manage any other production resource.

The operating lens changes the conversation. Instead of "we need more creative people," the question becomes "what conditions produce more creative output from the people we have?" The first question leads you to hiring. The second leads you to systems.

## The All Purpose thesis

All Purpose started with a conviction that creative output — for consumer products, for culture-adjacent work, for the kind of brand-building that actually compounds — had to be treated as a repeatable capability rather than an occasional event.

The practical expression of that was an attempt to systematise the inputs: the way briefs were written, the way ideas were reviewed, the way feedback moved through a team, the way distinct phases of work (exploration, selection, execution) were separated from each other. None of this is secret doctrine — it is what good creative studios have always done, under different names and in different arrangements. The point was to do it deliberately, and to be honest about what was working and what was not.

What emerged, slowly, was something that felt less like a creative agency and more like a creative operating system. A set of defaults that a team could run against, that could be improved based on evidence, that could be explained and taught and transferred to new people without losing the quality underneath.

That distinction — between creative culture and creative operating capacity — is the one worth holding onto. Culture is what people believe and how they behave. Operating capacity is what they can reliably produce. Both matter, but they are not the same, and you cannot substitute one for the other.

## What this means in practice

A team with strong creative culture but weak operating capacity produces good work inconsistently. The best ideas come when conditions align by accident. The rest of the time, output is lower than the team's potential. This is recognisable: the brilliant but unreliable creative organisation.

A team with strong operating capacity but weak culture produces consistent but thin work. The process runs, the output ships, but there is no juice in it. This is also recognisable: the efficient but forgettable operation.

What you are building toward — what was worth aiming at in the early work on All Purpose — is the third thing. Consistent, high-quality creative output that does not require exceptional conditions or exceptional people to generate it. That requires culture and operating capacity working together, neither substituting for the other.

The lever that most organisations miss is the operating side. Culture gets attention — values statements, hiring language, team rituals. Systems for managing creative output get far less. Yet the systems are where the consistency lives.

## The long game

None of this is quick work. Building creative operating capacity is slower than hiring a talented person and giving them room to run. It requires more honesty about where output is falling short and why. It requires the willingness to redesign the conditions rather than replace the people.

But it compounds in the way that most things worth building compound: slowly at first, then faster as the systems mature and the people who operate inside them understand what they are doing and why. Creative discipline, built over time, produces something that inspiration alone cannot — a reliable capacity to make things worth making, on demand, at scale, in the service of something real.

That is what we are trying to build. Not a company full of inspired people. A company with an operating system for creative work.
