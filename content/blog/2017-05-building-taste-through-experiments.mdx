## Taste is not something you have

There is a version of the word "taste" that functions as a credential. You either possess it or you don't. It belongs to people who were exposed to the right things early: the right schools, the right cities, the right rooms. By this account, taste is essentially inherited — a feature of upbringing, not effort. The implication is that some people have an eye for things, and the rest of us are working without one.

That account is wrong, and the wrongness of it has real consequences. If you believe taste is innate, you stop trying to develop it. You defer to people who seem to have it. You treat it as something mysterious, present in certain people and absent in others, and you exempt yourself from the slow, uncomfortable work of building it yourself.

The alternative view is less flattering but more useful: taste is built through a long sequence of experiments, each of which teaches you something, and none of which teaches you enough on its own. It accretes. It compounds over time. And the people who seem to have arrived with it fully formed are almost always people who started making things earlier, evaluated those things more honestly, and iterated more patiently than anyone around them noticed.

This has been the working assumption at Mustard Seed Group since the beginning, and May 2017 feels like a reasonable moment to make it explicit.

## What an experiment actually is

The word gets misused. In most product contexts, "experiment" means an A/B test. You run variant A against variant B, measure a conversion metric, and ship the winner. That is a reasonable engineering practice. It is not what we mean.

An experiment, in the sense we care about, is any act of making something and then honestly evaluating what came out. Both halves matter equally. The evaluation is where most people fail.

Most people make things and then defend them. This is natural — making something requires committing to it, and commitment generates attachment. The rare skill is to make something, sit with it, and see it clearly: what it does well, what it fails at, what assumption it reveals, what direction it points toward. Not to protect it. Not to dismiss it. To look at it.

You cannot shortcut the making. The evaluation is only meaningful if you have genuinely committed to the thing: built it to your honest best understanding, not to a safe minimum that can be disowned if it goes wrong. A half-committed experiment teaches you almost nothing, because you can always attribute the output to the effort rather than examine the idea. You need to have put enough of yourself into the thing that its failures are genuinely informative.

And you cannot shortcut the honesty. This is where the work is. There is a deep human pull toward protecting what you have already made. The portfolio is only as good as the group's willingness to evaluate its own work without flattering itself. That willingness is not automatic. It has to be practised, and practising it means building a tolerance for a specific kind of discomfort: seeing clearly something you built and finding it not good enough yet.

## How you build taste through this

The mechanism is not mysterious, though the timescale is longer than most people expect.

You make something. You look at it carefully. You notice what is off: the proportion that doesn't quite work, the copy that is trying too hard, the interaction that requires more explanation than it should require. You try to articulate why it is off. Not "something feels wrong" — that is a starting point, not a diagnosis. You push past the feeling toward a description: the hierarchy is unclear, the pacing is uneven, the call to action is earning distrust rather than confidence. Naming the problem is most of the work.

Then you make the next version with that articulation in mind. The next version surfaces a different set of problems. You articulate those. You keep going.

Over enough iterations, two things happen. First, you develop a vocabulary for what you are noticing. Problems that were once vague become specific. You can describe what is wrong rather than just feel it, which means you can address it deliberately rather than by luck.

Second, your instinct sharpens. Early on, you need to run through the checklist. Later, you see the problem immediately — before the checklist, before the rationalisation. That instinct is not magic: it is pattern recognition built from repeated, honest evaluation. The evaluation had to happen for the pattern to form. There is no way to acquire the pattern without doing the reps.

This is the feedback loop that builds taste. It requires volume: enough experiments that the patterns can emerge. It requires honesty: the willingness to see the work as it is, not as you wished it was. And it requires time. There is no compressed version of this process that produces the same result. You can accelerate the iteration cycle, but you cannot eliminate it.

## The three kinds of taste that matter here

Taste, broadly defined, covers a wide surface. Within the MSG portfolio, three distinct kinds show up most frequently, and they are related but not interchangeable.

**Product taste** is the ability to judge whether something works as a product: not whether it is technically sound, not whether users can figure it out with enough effort, but whether it earns its place in someone's workflow or life. Product taste is what you exercise when you cut a feature because it adds cognitive load without adding value. It is what you use when you decide that a simpler version of the thing — less polished, fewer options — actually does more for the person using it than a version loaded with capability they did not ask for.

Orbit is where this gets tested most directly. A B2B operating surface covering the full commercial workflow, from lead to launched product, is a sprawling thing to build. The surface area is large and the temptation to keep adding is constant. The only reliable check on sprawl is a consistent sense of what the product should and should not do — which requires product taste developed through building and evaluating many versions, each one clarifying what belongs and what doesn't.

**Creative taste** is the ability to judge whether something communicates: whether the thing you have made lands with the person it was made for, in the way you intended. This operates in visual design, in writing, in the structure of a conversation, in the rhythm of an interface. It is about intention matching outcome at the level of perception — whether the user receives what you were trying to send.

This matters in places you might not immediately expect. Naira, as a performance coach inside CheekyGains, is as much a creative challenge as it is a technical one. An AI coach that communicates poorly is worse than useless — it actively undermines trust, and trust is the entire basis on which coaching works. Getting the voice right, the timing right, the level of directness right: these are creative decisions, not configuration decisions. Making them well requires a body of creative experiments behind you, not just a model trained on the right data.

**Commercial taste** is the ability to judge which ideas are worth pursuing at all: not just whether they work in principle, but whether they will hold up in a real market with real constraints and real alternatives. This is the hardest to develop because the feedback loop is the slowest. You make something, you take it to market, and months pass before you know what you are actually dealing with. By the time you have a clear signal, you have already made dozens of downstream decisions on the basis of incomplete information.

TUXX operates in this space most directly. Building custom AI systems and software for clients means making judgment calls constantly: what patterns are worth productising across clients, what requests are genuinely commercial versus one-off engagements, what the difference is between a client who will stretch your thinking and a client who will dilute it. These calls require commercial taste, and commercial taste requires having made a lot of bets and lived with the outcomes — including the ones that were wrong.

## Why the portfolio benefits from all three

The three kinds of taste are related but not identical. Someone with sharp product taste may be a poor judge of commercial viability. Someone with excellent commercial instinct may not be able to see why a specific design decision undermines the product's trustworthiness at the moment of use. And creative taste, without the others, can produce work that is admired but not adopted.

One case for running multiple products across multiple categories is cross-pollination of feedback. What the creative work inside All Purpose teaches about communication, rhythm, and what resonates with an audience feeds back into how Orbit surfaces information for a commercial team. What the commercial reality of TUXX client work teaches about what organisations actually need — versus what they say they need — informs what Benediction Lab decides is worth researching. What Benediction Lab learns about agent behaviour and memory systems becomes material for what Orbit can eventually offer.

The experiments are not isolated. They run in parallel across a portfolio, and the feedback from each one is potentially useful to all the others. The compound effect of this, if the evaluation is honest and the connections are actually made, is a group that develops taste faster than any single-product studio could. You are not just iterating on one thing — you are building a shared vocabulary of what works across multiple surfaces and multiple contexts simultaneously.

## The discipline this requires

None of this runs on goodwill. The experiments only teach what they teach if the evaluation happens with some rigour.

This means establishing clear criteria for what you were trying to do before you assess whether you did it. Retrofitting the target after you have seen the result is not evaluation — it is rationalisation. It means separating "I don't like this" from "this does not serve its purpose." Personal preference is data, but it is not the whole story. And it means sitting with failures long enough to understand them rather than discarding them as quickly as possible and moving on.

It also means accepting that most experiments will not work as hoped. This is not a failure of the process — it is the process working correctly. The experiments that go wrong are often the most instructive. A version that nearly worked but missed in a particular way tells you something specific about where the judgment was off. A version that failed entirely tells you something about the initial assumptions. Both kinds of information are valuable. Neither is available if you do not make the thing.

The working thesis at MSG has always been that capability is built, not found. That applies to taste as much as anything else. The difference between where we are and where we want to be is not a matter of waiting for the right moment, the right collaborators, or the right market conditions. It is a matter of making things, seeing them clearly, and adjusting. Repeatedly, and with patience.

That is the work.
