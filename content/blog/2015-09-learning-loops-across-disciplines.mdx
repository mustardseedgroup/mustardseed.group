## The question that started this

What would happen if the people building the product sat in the room whilst the coaches were running a session?

Not to observe, not to take notes in a formal sense — just to be present. To watch what works, what gets ignored, what creates genuine behaviour change and what fades the moment the session ends.

We tried it. The answer surprised us, and it has shaped how Mustard Seed Group thinks about learning ever since.

## Why disciplines hoard their insights

Every discipline develops a body of knowledge that is, for the most part, kept inside the discipline. Coaches talk to coaches. Product designers talk to product designers. Commercial strategists talk to commercial strategists. The conferences, the books, the vocabulary — all of it reinforces the internal logic of the domain and, without meaning to, keeps the useful parts from travelling.

This is not arrogance. It is partly efficiency — you build shared language so you can think faster — and partly the natural shape of expertise, which demands deep investment in a single area before it pays back. But it has a cost. The most transferable insights are often the ones nobody thinks to transfer, because inside the discipline they feel obvious.

The performance coach who has spent years learning how to build accountability structures into a training programme has solved a genuinely hard problem. The difficulty is that the solution rarely makes it into a conversation about product design, even though the problems are structurally identical. How do you keep someone engaged with a system after the initial motivation fades? How do you make progress legible enough to sustain effort? How do you build a relationship between the person and the system that holds during the difficult stretches?

These questions belong as much to software as they do to sport.

## What we noticed moving between contexts

Mustard Seed Group operates across creative, technical and commercial contexts, not because that mix was planned from the start but because the problems we care about tend to sit at the intersection of all three. When you are building a product that has to earn its place in someone's working life, you need commercial instinct, technical literacy and enough creative intelligence to understand what the thing actually is — what experience it creates, not just what functions it performs.

The learning loop we stumbled into was this: things that we learned in one context started informing decisions in another, often weeks or months later, in ways that would not have happened if we had kept those activities in separate boxes.

A lesson about feedback in performance coaching — specifically about when to give it and when to let silence do the work — turned out to be directly applicable to how an interface communicates progress. A lesson about the commercial dynamics of a creative pitch — why stories close the room better than spreadsheets — turned out to be applicable to how a product explains itself before anyone opens the pricing page. A technical discovery about how people actually navigate a system versus how we assumed they would navigate it turned out to be applicable to how a coaching framework was being delivered.

None of these transfers were obvious in advance. They only became visible once we created the conditions for them.

## Creating the conditions

A deliberate cross-disciplinary learning loop requires three things that do not happen by default.

The first is physical or temporal proximity. You cannot learn across disciplines from a distance. At some point the people operating in different domains have to be in the same room, working on adjacent problems, noticing what the other person is doing. This is uncomfortable because it requires a degree of vulnerability — if you are a software engineer watching a coaching session, you are operating outside your domain, which means you cannot perform expertise in the usual way.

The second is a shared vocabulary that is neutral enough to carry insights from one side to the other without distorting them. This is harder than it sounds. Disciplines borrow words from each other and change their meanings in transit — "feedback", "iteration", "system" — all of these carry very different technical implications depending on context. Building a working vocabulary means accepting that the early conversations will be imprecise, and that imprecision is doing useful work.

The third is time. Cross-disciplinary learning is slow by the standards of single-discipline mastery. A coaching principle does not arrive in a product conversation as a finished idea. It arrives as a vague sense that something relevant has been encountered, which has to be thought about, discussed, tentatively applied and then evaluated. Organisations that are moving fast in a single lane will not tolerate this. Organisations that understand it as an investment will eventually find that the slow transfers compound.

## The MSG portfolio as a deliberate structure

Looking at how Mustard Seed Group is organised at this point in September 2015, the portfolio structure is not accidental. We are not pursuing a diversified holding strategy in the financial sense. We are building a system in which different types of work produce learning that is deliberately meant to flow across the whole.

The commercial and services work — the client-facing engagements, the custom systems work — is not just a revenue line. It is a way of encountering real operational problems in real organisations, under real time pressure, at a frequency that product development alone cannot produce. That encounter generates specific, grounded lessons about what fails in practice, what humans will actually tolerate, and where the gap between designed intent and lived experience tends to be widest.

The consumer fitness and performance work generates a different kind of lesson: what drives sustained engagement at the individual level, how accountability structures hold over time, what the emotional texture of progress and stagnation looks like from the inside. These are questions that B2B product design often ignores because B2B products assume rational, motivated users operating in professional contexts. The consumer experience corrects that assumption almost immediately.

The technical research work — the quieter, slower strand of the portfolio — generates lessons about what becomes possible before it is commercially viable, which means it can shape the direction of the other work before the market does. If you only learn from commercial feedback, you always learn slightly too late.

## The asymmetry that matters

There is an asymmetry worth naming directly. The lessons that flow from creative and performance work into commercial and technical work tend to be more surprising to the recipient than the reverse. Commercial and technical people are accustomed to having their insights valued and circulated. Performance and creative insights often get received with polite interest and then quietly set aside, because their mechanism is less legible.

But "less legible" does not mean "less real." The reason a coaching method works is often not the obvious reason — it works because of something structural in how the relationship is built, or because of a specific timing decision, or because of what is deliberately left unaddressed. These structural patterns are exactly the kind of thing that transfers well, once you learn to look for them in the right register.

Making those patterns legible enough to travel across disciplines is genuinely difficult work. It requires someone willing to hold the translation project — to sit in both rooms, to carry the half-formed insight until it develops into something usable, to resist the pressure to conclude too quickly.

## What this is building towards

Mustard Seed Group is, at its core, a capability-building institution. The products are the outputs, but the actual work is developing the capacity to understand problems deeply enough to build things that deserve to exist.

Cross-disciplinary learning loops are not a nice-to-have in that project. They are structural. The insight that a performance lesson can inform a product decision is not a clever analogy — it is a claim about the underlying shape of human systems, which tend to have more in common across surface contexts than the surface contexts suggest.

We are still early in understanding how to manage this deliberately rather than accidentally. The moments when the learning actually transfers are still more often serendipitous than planned. But the shape of the thing is becoming clearer: build the portfolio so that different kinds of work are always in contact; invest in the translators and the translation time; value the slow, imprecise insight that crosses a boundary over the fast, precise insight that stays inside one.

That is the loop. It is not efficient by conventional measures. But it is the one we are most committed to sustaining.
